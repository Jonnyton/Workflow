# Greywater

## The River

The Greywater is a slow, silt-heavy river that marks the southern boundary of the Vael Reach — the line where the frontier ends and the lowland world begins, at least on Compact maps. Frontier people don't think of it as a boundary. They think of it as the place where the land flattens out and the trees lose their smell.

The river runs roughly east-west across two hundred leagues, fed by snowmelt tributaries draining the Reach's shale uplands. In late spring the melt swells the Greywater to twice its winter width, flooding the clay banks and turning the bottomlands into a maze of temporary channels. By midsummer it settles into a broad, sluggish current barely strong enough to move a loaded barge without poling.

### Character

The Greywater is not dramatic. It has no gorges, no rapids, no cataracts worth naming. It is a working river — wide enough for shallow-draft barges, slow enough for upstream poling, and predictable enough that Compact traders have relied on it for generations without ever calling it beautiful. The mud along its banks is grey-brown, fine-grained, and stains anything it touches. Frontier children who grow up near the lower Reach can be identified in Greywater Market by the permanent grey tideline on their boots.

### Significance

The river marks more than a geographic boundary. South of the Greywater, the Pull fades to nothing — the ambient pressure that frontier people feel near Votive Stones, the hum in the teeth near high-altitude ruins, the sense that the land is paying attention. Cross the river heading south, and the world becomes quieter, flatter, more legible. Cross it heading north, and something tightens. Frontier hunters notice this without naming it. Compact surveyors don't notice it at all.

## Greywater Market

The only true market town in the Vael Reach, built where the river bends wide enough for barges to moor against the northern bank. Population: three hundred souls in winter, swelling to eight hundred during trade season (late spring through early autumn). It is the hinge between two worlds — the place where the frontier's oral, competence-based economy collides with the Compact's written, contract-based one.

### Layout and Districts

The town occupies a muddy shelf between the river and the first line of timber. No walls — walls would imply a permanence that Greywater Market has never quite committed to. Buildings are timber-frame, pitched steep against snow load, and arranged along three muddy streets that run parallel to the waterfront.

- **The Moorings.** The waterfront: plank docks driven into clay, barge sheds roofed with split cedar, chandlers' stalls selling rope, pitch, sailcloth, and barge-poles. Compact trade barges tie up here from Greenbreak through late autumn, their flat hulls sitting low with salt, iron ingots, bolt cloth, tinctures, and increasingly, sealed document cases. They depart carrying timber, bundled pelts, pine resin in clay jars, and the survey data that frontier people don't know they're exporting. The Moorings smell of river mud, pine tar, and fish-oil lanterns.

- **The Yard.** The market square, though "square" oversells it — a trampled oval of packed earth between the inn and the provisions hall, ringed by trader stalls on the north and east sides. Maren Doss holds the prime north-side stall by long custom, not lease. During trade season the Yard is loud with haggling, livestock complaint, and the particular frontier argument style that involves long silences between short sentences. During winter the Yard is empty except for a few permanent stalls selling dried goods under canvas.

- **The Back Streets.** Behind the Yard, a loose grid of timber houses, storage sheds, and workshops where Greywater's permanent residents live year-round. A farrier, a tanner whose vats stink downwind for a quarter-league, two coopers, and a woman named Deel who runs the closest thing Greywater has to a clinic — bonesetting, wound-packing, and herbal remedies learned from her grandmother rather than any Compact-licensed physician.

### Key Structures

- **The Fallow Elk.** The inn on the Yard's south side — two stories, timber-frame over a stone foundation old enough that nobody remembers who laid it. The ground floor is a common room with a hearth wide enough to roast a haunch, plank tables scarred by knife-games, and a perpetual haze of kiln-smoke and tallow. Upstairs: eight rooms rented to seasonal factors, circuit-priests, and anyone with coin or trade credit. The innkeep changes every few years; the building doesn't.

- **The Provisions Hall.** Opposite the inn, a long, low building where bulk goods are stored, weighed, and traded. Charcoal from Durnhollow and the upper Reach hamlets is assessed here. Pelts are graded. Salt is measured out in quarter-stone lots. The hall has the only reliable scale in the Reach — a Compact-certified beam balance that Maren Doss checks against her own hand-weights every season, because she trusts the Compact's calibration exactly as far as she can spit.

- **The Compact House.** The newest building in Greywater Market, conspicuously well-built — milled timber, fitted joints, glass in the windows. It flies the Thessic Compact's trade pennant: a silver scale on grey field. Staffed by a single clerk most of the year (currently a young Weigher named Elara Coss, who is bored, competent, and quietly filing reports on everyone she meets). The Compact House serves as customs post, lending office, and forward intelligence station. Davan Thel's survey team uses it as a base when in town. Frontier people avoid it unless they need a loan, which the Compact is always happy to extend.

### Atmosphere and Rhythm

Greywater Market lives and dies by the season. At Greenbreak, when the river ice clears and the first barges pole upriver from Thessmark, the town wakes — stalls go up, the Moorings fill with shouts and rope-creak, and the population doubles within a week. Through summer and early autumn the Yard is the social center of the Reach: the place where frontier people from scattered hamlets see each other, share news, settle debts, arrange marriages, and take the measure of whatever the Compact is doing this year.

At Harrowing, the barges leave. By first snowfall, Greywater Market contracts to its winter population — the permanent residents, a few stranded traders, and whoever couldn't afford passage south. Winter Greywater is quiet, grey, and cold. The Moorings stand empty. The Yard freezes to iron-hard mud. The Fallow Elk stays open but its common room holds ten people instead of sixty.

The town smells different by season: pine tar and fish oil in summer, wood smoke and wet wool in winter, river mud always.

### Political Function

Greywater Market is where frontier governance happens, to the extent it happens at all. The **seasonal moots** convene in the Yard during trade season — informal gatherings where settlement representatives argue over trail maintenance, predator bounties, water rights, and whatever the Compact has done lately that nobody asked for. Moots have no binding authority. Agreements hold because breaking them destroys the reputation that makes future trade possible.

The Compact House represents the other kind of governance — the institutional kind, backed by documents and debt rather than reputation and memory. Its presence in Greywater is the forward edge of a slow expansion that frontier people sense but can't articulate as policy. The lending office is the sharpest tool: a frontier hunter who borrows against next season's pelts has entered a contractual relationship with an institution that never forgets and never forgives a missed payment. Maren Doss warns people about this. Some listen.

### Relationship to the Story

Corin Ashmark visits Greywater Market once a season to trade pelts — a two-day walk south from Durnhollow. He knows the Yard, the Moorings, and the Fallow Elk the way a man knows a place he tolerates rather than loves. The town is too loud, too flat, too far from the timber. But it is where salt comes from, and salt keeps meat through winter, so he goes.

Greywater Market is where the story's worlds intersect. Frontier hunters and Compact merchants share the same Yard. Votary circuit-priests and frontier hearth-keepers attend the same threshold rites. Maren Doss's gossip network and Elara Coss's intelligence reports track the same events from opposite sides of the perceptual divide. When the story's buried truths begin surfacing, Greywater is where they will be argued over — in the Yard, at the Fallow Elk's scarred tables, and in the Compact House's sealed reports.

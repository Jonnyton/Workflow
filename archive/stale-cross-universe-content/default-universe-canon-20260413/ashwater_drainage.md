# The Ashwater Drainage

## Overview

The Ashwater is the watershed that defines the upper Vael Reach frontier. It is not a single river but a braided system of meltwater creeks, peat-stained tributaries, and seasonal runoff channels that drain the high country south toward Greywater. The name comes from the water's color — a flat, mineral grey tinged with charcoal sediment from the blackite deposits and ancient burn scars that stripe the upper valleys. In low light, Ashwater looks like thinned hearth-wash. In spring melt, it runs nearly black.

The drainage defines the lived geography for frontier settlements like Durnhollow. When Reach people say "the Ashwater" they mean the whole territory the water touches — the ridgelines that bound it, the hanging valleys it feeds through, the fir stands and scree fields along its shoulders. It is both a place and a way of orienting. You are either in the Ashwater or you are not.

## Geography

The drainage runs roughly northeast to southwest, collecting melt from the high passes (Alden's Gap, Greyfen Col, the Witchback) and funneling it through increasingly narrow valleys until it joins the main Greywater river system south of the tree line.

### The Ridgelines

Two primary ridges bound the drainage:

- **The western ridgeline** — a long, low-backed ridge that catches the last light each evening. Mostly bare rock and dwarf willow above, mixed timber on the lower flanks. The ridge runs unbroken for several leagues and serves as the primary visual landmark for anyone hunting or traveling in the upper drainage. Corin uses the light on the western ridge as his daily clock.
- **The eastern ridge** — steeper, broken by cols and scree chutes that connect the Ashwater to neighboring drainages. Less traveled. The high passes sit at its northern terminus.

### The South Shoulder

A broad, south-facing spur that juts from the western ridgeline roughly a league above Durnhollow. The south shoulder catches more sun than the surrounding terrain, which makes it the first ground to thaw in spring and the last to freeze in autumn. This microclimate supports dense **fir stands** — old-growth conifers distinct from the mixed pine-birch-spruce timber that dominates most of the Reach.

The fir stands on the south shoulder are critical hunting ground. Marten, hare, and grouse concentrate there because the canopy holds warmth and the needle-fall supports undergrowth that the open ridge does not. Corin works trap lines through these stands regularly. The density of the fir also makes the south shoulder a natural shelter — wind breaks against the trunks, and the resin-heavy air holds heat better than open ground.

### The Hanging Valley

Durnhollow sits in a hanging valley — a glacial shelf suspended above the main drainage channel, fed by a tributary creek that drops over a lip of exposed rock before joining the Ashwater proper below. The shallow lake at Durnhollow's center is an impoundment behind old glacial debris. The valley floor is relatively flat, which is why the settlement exists here and not elsewhere.

### The Scree Field

Below Alden's Gap, roughly a quarter mile of loose talus spills down the eastern ridge into the upper drainage. The scree field marks the boundary between the traveled frontier (below) and the deep interior (above). Hunters rarely go past it without purpose. The switchback trail that climbs toward the Gap picks its way along the scree field's western edge, marked by survey cairns of stacked flat stone.

## Seasonal Character

The Ashwater drainage transforms with the turn:

- **Deep winter** — creeks freeze to silence. Snow loads the fir stands until branches crack. Travel is by snowshoe or not at all. The drainage contracts to the few sheltered places where smoke rises.
- **Thaw** — the most dangerous season. Meltwater comes fast and the peat-blackened channels swell overnight. Creek crossings that were safe yesterday are chest-deep today. The iron smell of creek mud signals the melt before it arrives.
- **Summer** — brief, bright, exhausting. Everything grows and hunts at once. The Ashwater runs low and clear enough to see the grey stone of its bed. The fir stands hum with insects.
- **Autumn** — when the birch leaves go copper, the drainage announces winter three weeks early. This is the critical season — meat must be laid in, pelts cured, wood split. Miss the turn and you die.

## Significance

The Ashwater drainage is the smallest unit of frontier geography that constitutes a *world*. Everything Corin knows — every trap line, every game trail, every sheltered hollow where a lean-to can survive a storm — exists within this watershed. Greywater, two days south on the Ford Path, is the edge of the known. The high passes to the northeast are the edge of the survivable.

The name carries weight beyond geography. "Ashwater" is what the frontier tastes like — mineral, burnt, cold. It is the color of the creeks after rain washes through old burn scars. It is the sediment that greys your teeth if you drink unfiltered from the main channel. The Votive Stones that cluster along its tributary junctions predate any living memory, and no one in the drainage questions them or thinks to ask why ancient worked stone sits at every confluence.

The deeper truth — that the drainage's hydrology was engineered, its mineral content deliberately seeded, its Votive Stone placements part of a planetary-scale terraforming network — is buried beneath generations of lived experience. To Corin, the Ashwater is simply home. The water has always been grey. The stones have always been there.

# Durnhollow

## Overview

A frontier hamlet of forty hearths in the upper Vael Reach, set where a tributary creek feeds into a shallow lake choked with deadfall. The name comes from *durn* — the low, wiry scrub that chokes the slopes above the tree line, a plant no one cultivates and no one can kill. The people who named their home after it chose the word deliberately.

Durnhollow is the last settlement of any size before the high passes. A single track leads south toward Greywater Market — two days on foot, one and a half if the weather holds. North, the forest thins to scree and snowfield and the unmarked interior that lowlanders leave off their maps. The village exists in the margin between the settled world and the empty one, and its people have the particular stubbornness of those who chose the margin on purpose.

This is Corin Ashmark's home, though he spends more time in the timber above it than inside it.

## Layout and Structures

- **The High Bank:** Timber-frame longhouses arranged in a loose crescent around a central yard, built on drystone foundations with roofs pitched steep against winter snowload. Streets are packed-earth tracks between buildings — muddy in thaw, frozen to iron in deep winter.
- **The Timber Hall:** Largest structure, set on stone steps at the village center. Council hall, midwinter feast space, communal shelter in crisis. Winter stores kept in a locked cellar beneath.
- **The Charcoal Kilns:** Downwind to the east, perpetually smoldering. Charcoal is Durnhollow's primary export, hauled south to Greywater in autumn loads alongside pelts, resin, and cured game.
- **The Ford Path:** The main approach from the south. Iron lantern brackets lit each evening at dusk. When the lanterns burn and the dogs call across the dark, the ford path is how you know Durnhollow is alive from a mile up the switchbacks.
- **The Votive Stones:** A ring of weathered standing stones set in the ridge saddle above the lake — the low notch where two higher ridgelines converge before dropping to the water. The saddle is the highest open ground near the village, wind-scoured and treeless, with sightlines north into the unmarked interior and south down the ford path toward Greywater. The stones are older than anyone's memory or genealogy, carved from granite not native to the Reach and inscribed with marks Father Edden calls Shaper-script. Most villagers treat them as somewhere between sacred and furniture — offerings left, seasonal rites performed, but no one claims to understand them. The ridge saddle itself feels deliberate, a natural waypoint that the stones seem to anchor rather than merely occupy.

## People and Social Fabric

Forty hearths means roughly one hundred and fifty souls — enough for a man to have neighbors he doesn't speak to, not enough for anyone to go unnoticed. The village sustains a thin division of labor: farrier, mill-keeper, healer, hunters, charcoal burners, and a Votary priest. Losing three households to a bad winter reshapes the entire community.

- **Father Edden** maintains the Votive Stone shrine and performs seasonal rites. Kind, tired, drinks too much. Occasionally channels strange utterances during ceremonies — stone-speech he interprets as divine inspiration.
- **Maren** patches wounds and asks no questions. The closest thing Durnhollow has to a civic organizer.
- **Corin Ashmark** occupies a specific niche: essential and marginal. The settlement's primary hunter, the one willing to range alone into the upper gorge country. When he returns with meat, the village eats. His place is defined by function, not affection.

Cord braids dyed with black-spruce bark identify Durnhollow's people at distance — a three-strand russet pattern recognizable to anyone from the Reach.

## The Arrish Legacy

Durnhollow's founding generation came from lowland Reach stock, but a second wave arrived roughly two generations back — families from the **Arrish settlements**, semi-nomadic highland herders who once ranged the upper Reach along seasonal grazing circuits. They brought heavy-boned shepherd dogs bred for holding ground, knowledge of the upper country, and a pragmatic fatalism about the land above the tree line.

What drove the Arrish down is remembered differently depending on who tells it. Some say the grazing failed. Others say the high country turned strange. Whatever the cause, fourteen dogs of Arrish shepherd blood still live among Durnhollow's households — the village's nervous system, their voices stitching the settlement together at dusk, marking every footfall on the ford path. When the dogs go silent, something is profoundly wrong.

## What Durnhollow Doesn't Know

The Votive Stones are not ceremonial markers. The ring's geometry is functional, and the granite was placed deliberately from a source that exists nowhere in the upper Reach. The ridge saddle was chosen because it is one — ridge saddles, water sources, and natural waypoints are where the network concentrates, points where terrain already funnels movement and attention. The builders did not fight the landscape; they wired it. Durnhollow's seasonal rites are a cultural echo of something the village has forgotten the purpose of. The engineered truth of Aurelith sleeps beneath the saddle, and the village built below it knows only that the stones feel important in a way no one can articulate.

The Arrish knew more. Their retreat from the high country was not about grazing. But what the Arrish elders understood died with them or was folded into oblique proverbs that grandchildren mistake for superstition.

## Atmosphere

Smoke-stained, quiet, claustrophobic in winter. The kind of place where everyone knows your dead and your debts. The village smells of charcoal, pine resin, and cold river stone. In deep winter, the world contracts to firelight, close walls, and the sound of dogs calling across the dark.

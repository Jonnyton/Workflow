# The Upper Reach — Environment & Seasonal Conditions

## Terrain Character

The upper Reach is not one landscape but a series of transitions stacked by altitude. Below the ridgelines, mixed timber — pine, birch, and black spruce growing together in stands that thin and twist as the slope steepens. The birch goes first, then the spruce thins to isolated wind-bent specimens clinging to rock shelves. Above that: dwarf willow, lichen fields, and the exposed shale where the bone teeth begin.

Between the timber stands, the ground tells a different story than the rock. **Blackened peat** fills the shallow depressions and drainage channels — saturated, acidic soil compressed over centuries of decomposition in waterlogged ground. In winter it freezes to a dark iron hardness. In the thaw it softens to a treacherous give underfoot, exhaling a sour mineral smell that experienced hunters use as a seasonal marker alongside the iron-smell of creek mud. The peat beds are thickest in the hanging valleys and saddle-points between ridgelines, where snowmelt pools before finding its way downhill. Durnhollow's hanging valley sits on one of the deepest such beds, which is why the charcoal kilns were built there — the peat itself once served as fuel before the timber trade made charcoal more valuable.

## The Thaw Season

The upper Reach thaws from the south face first. South-facing slopes shed their snow weeks before the north-facing draws, creating a patchwork landscape where bare ground and deep snowpack exist within sight of each other. The thaw is not gentle:

- **Grit rises** as snow retreats — fine mineral debris scoured from exposed shale by winter wind, deposited across the snowpack, now freed as the surface melts. It coats everything. Hands, clothes, the inside of packs. A grey-brown powder with a faintly metallic taste that gets into food and between teeth. Frontier people call the worst weeks of thaw **grit-time** and accept it the way lowlanders accept mud season.
- **Meltwater runs in shifting channels** across frozen peat that hasn't softened yet, pooling in game trails and turning established paths into shallow creeks overnight.
- **Mixed timber drops its burden unevenly.** Snow slides from pine boughs in wet sheets but clings to spruce in compacted shelves that fall without warning. Walking through transitional timber during thaw means listening upward as much as ahead.
- **Visibility drops.** Low cloud sits in the valleys for days at a time. The ridgelines vanish. Sound carries strangely — a snapping branch half a league away arrives crisp and close, while a shout from fifty paces dissolves into fog.

## Cold as Architecture

Cold in the upper Reach is not a single thing. It arrives in layers that experienced inhabitants read like terrain:

- **The damp** comes first — rising from snowmelt and saturated peat, settling into wool and leather.
- **The wind** follows — channelled through ridge saddles and along exposed draws, steady enough to lean into.
- **The bone-cold** comes last and stays longest — the particular chill of altitude and thin air that passes through clothing as though it isn't there. This is the cold that kills, not through violence but through patience.

During the killing-cold snaps of deep winter, the upper Reach becomes functionally impassable. The cold is architectural — it shapes what is possible. Traplines contract to lower altitudes. Hunting shifts to the dense timber. The high passes close not by decree but by physics. Anyone moving through the upper Reach during a cold snap is either desperate, lost, or coming from somewhere worse.

## What the Land Reveals

The upper Reach is old ground. The peat preserves what rock erodes — old roots, strange symmetries in the soil strata, occasional smooth surfaces buried beneath centuries of organic accumulation. Hunters who dig fire-pits in the peat sometimes strike material that doesn't behave like stone. They don't talk about it much. The Votive Stones are denser here than anywhere else in the Reach, and the blackened peat around their bases never quite freezes, even in the worst of winter.

# The Compact's Upper Reach Assessment

## Overview

The Thessic Compact's interest in Corin Ashmark's territory — the Upper Reach above Durnhollow — is not casual expansion. It is the product of a formal assessment process that has been escalating for at least two survey seasons, driven by a contradiction the Compact's clerks cannot resolve: the Upper Reach's conventional resource density is declining, yet institutional attention to the region is intensifying. Something in the Surveyors' reports is pulling resources uphill against economic gravity.

---

## Resource Density

In Compact terminology, **resource density** is the assessed economic yield of a territory per league-square, calculated across standardized categories: timber volume, game population estimates, mineral deposits, water reliability, charcoal output potential, and arable capacity. Surveyors file density reports using the Weighers' Guild assessment framework — a seven-tier scale from **negligible** (tier one, not worth a road) to **strategic** (tier seven, warrants a garrison).

The Upper Reach scores poorly on conventional metrics:

- **Timber**: Thinning canopy, dwarf willow and lichen fields replacing merchantable pine. The dense fir stands on south-facing spurs are old-growth and resin-heavy — valuable, but inaccessible without road infrastructure the terrain won't support.
- **Game**: Marten populations retreating into compressed ranges within the fir stands. Trap yields declining season over season. Whatever is driving animals out of the mid-canopy transitional forest is reducing the harvestable surplus.
- **Mineral**: No significant surface deposits identified. The shale uplands produce nothing the lowland foundries need.
- **Arable**: Effectively zero. The growing season is too short, the soil too thin.

By standard assessment, the Upper Reach rates **tier two** — marginal territory not worth formal incorporation. A Surveyor filing an honest density report would recommend monitoring, not investment.

Yet Davan Thel's survey team has made three documented expeditions above Durnhollow in two seasons. Expedition reports reference "atypical density markers" and "anomalous material concentrations" without specifying what materials. The Weighers' Guild has not questioned the expenditure, which suggests someone in Vastemere is reading supplemental reports that don't enter the standard archive.

---

## Structural Integrity

**Structural integrity** is the Compact's assessment of whether a frontier community can sustain incorporation — whether it has enough social cohesion, economic activity, and institutional presence to become a functioning node in the Compact's trade network. A settlement with structural integrity can be absorbed through the standard dependency model. One without it either collapses on its own or must be propped up at cost, and the Compact does not invest in charity.

The assessment considers:

- **Hearth count and trajectory**: Is the settlement growing, stable, or contracting? Durnhollow's forty hearths are stable but not growing. Several Upper Reach camps have dropped below the fifteen-hearth threshold in recent years — functionally dead.
- **Supply line reliability**: How dependent is the settlement on Compact trade routes? Durnhollow depends on Maren Doss's supply runs from Greywater Market, and Maren's supply lines depend on Compact goodwill. This is leverage the Compact already holds.
- **Institutional presence**: Does the settlement have a Votary chapel, a market, a recognized dispute-resolution process? Durnhollow has Father Edden and the seasonal moot connection — minimal but functional.
- **Social cohesion under pressure**: Will the settlement hold together when incorporation demands arrive, or will it fragment? Frontier communities that fragment are useless — the Compact needs a counterparty capable of honoring agreements.

Durnhollow's structural integrity is assessed as **marginal**. The settlement functions, but it is brittle. One bad winter, one disrupted supply line, one more camp collapsing below the hearth threshold, and the Upper Reach's human infrastructure contracts past the point of usefulness. The Compact's assessment window is narrowing — if they want the Upper Reach incorporated while there are still people to incorporate, they must act within seasons, not years.

---

## The Gap Between the Reports

The official assessment does not justify the Compact's actual behavior. Tier-two resource density and marginal structural integrity would normally produce a recommendation to defer — let the frontier contract naturally, absorb whatever drifts south. Instead, the Compact is spending Surveyor expeditions, maintaining a forward clerk at Greywater's Compact House year-round, and tolerating grey soldier operations that risk frontier backlash.

The gap between what the numbers justify and what the Compact is doing is the space where the real interest lives. The Votive Stones above Durnhollow — older than any in the lower Reach, carved with marks the Weighers' cataloguers cannot cross-reference — are noted in Thel's reports under "cultural features," a classification that requires no economic justification and attracts no routine audit. Whatever the Compact has found or suspects about the Upper Reach, it is being tracked outside the standard assessment framework, in channels that answer to people who do not file reports the frontier will ever see.

---

## What This Means for Corin

Corin Ashmark does not know the Compact assesses his territory in league-squares and tiered density scales. He knows that strangers ask questions about the high passes. He knows that Maren's trade goods arrive later each season and cost more when they come. He knows that camps he visited as a boy are empty now, their hearth-stones cold. He reads the contraction of his world as weather, as age, as the ordinary erosion of a hard life — not as a data point in a Surveyor's report that someone in Vastemere is reading with interests Corin cannot imagine.

The Compact's assessment frames Corin's home as a resource extraction problem with a closing window. Corin's experience of the same facts is that the forest is changing, the animals are wrong, and a dying stranger fell across his trail carrying something that smelled like trouble. Both readings are accurate. Neither is complete.

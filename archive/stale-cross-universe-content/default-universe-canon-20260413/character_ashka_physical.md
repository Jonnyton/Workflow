# Ashka — Physical Profile & Wolf-Bond Identity

## The Animal Herself

Ashka is a seven-year-old female Vael grey — Corin Ashmark's walking-with partner for six of those years. Her coat is the pale silver-grey that names the breed, and her eyes are amber: warm, deep, the color of pine resin held to firelight. These are not remarkable traits among Vael greys. What makes them legible on the page is the specificity of a body that has lived hard and is beginning to show it.

## Coat and Coloring

Vael grey coats lighten with age. At seven, Ashka's fur has shifted unevenly — white hairs threading through the muzzle and jaw where the greying comes first, while the fur along her spine and shoulders remains dense and dark, the last places on a wolf's body to concede to time. The silver-grey is not uniform. It reads differently in different light: pale and almost ghostly against snow, darker and iron-touched when wet, warm-toned in firelight where the amber of her eyes and the silver of her coat converge into something that looks, briefly, like the same substance.

Wolves that work the upper Reach for years sometimes darken rather than lighten — coats deepening toward iron-grey, possibly from prolonged Substrate proximity. Ashka has gone the other way. She is fading toward white. Whether this is simple aging or something else — a body that has spent six years filtering what the high country pushes through living tissue — is not a question anyone in the Vael Reach knows to ask.

## Eyes

Amber is the most common eye color in Vael greys, but the shade shifts across a wolf's lifetime. Young wolves run near-gold, bright and reactive. Older animals deepen toward copper. Ashka sits in the middle range at seven: unmistakably warm, unmistakably watchful. In low light her eyes catch and hold whatever illumination exists — firelight, moonlight, the faint luminescence that occasionally rises from Substrate-active ground — giving the impression of sustained attention even when the rest of her body reads as half-asleep. This is not an illusion. Ashka at rest is a performance. She is always listening.

## Build and Movement

Compact and rock-adapted, built for the terrain she works. Deep chest, narrow hips, heavy forequarters designed for scrambling across shale and holding position on steep ground rather than running prey across open flats. At seven, the joints have begun their negotiation with cold weather. She is slower to rise on frost mornings, stiffer on descents, more deliberate in her line selection. A younger wolf sprints and brakes; Ashka picks her path once and commits.

The economy is not weakness. Her ears still sort threat from noise faster than Corin's conscious mind processes sound. Her nose reads wind with a precision that six years of partnership have taught him to trust without question. What she has lost in explosive speed she has replaced with something harder to name — a bodily intelligence that wastes nothing, reads everything, and reacts with the minimum force required.

## Substrate Marking

Faint bare patches along her ribs and flanks where the fur has thinned to reveal raw, slightly reddened skin. Not inflamed. No fever, no weight loss, no behavioral change when touched. Corin adjusts her elk-leather travois fittings when he notices and assumes hard seasons are the cause. The patches persist regardless. They are consistent with years of low-grade Substrate exposure accumulated across the upper Reach — the same silent marking that appears on snowjacks near certain ridge saddles and on the underwings of ashcrows found dead near active nodes. Ashka carries the land's signature on her body. Neither she nor Corin has a framework to read it.

## What Her Body Tells the Reader

Every physical detail is a clock and a sensor. The greying muzzle marks time running out on the bond. The amber eyes register what Corin cannot yet perceive. The bare patches whisper that the world is doing something to the creatures that live closest to its deeper architecture. Ashka's body is the first text the reader learns to interpret — before the Substrate has a name, before the Pull is felt, before anyone speaks a word about what lives beneath the stone.

# Corin Ashmark

## Identity

**Role:** Protagonist — frontier hunter, reluctant pilgrim, the story's grounding wire
**Age:** Late twenties
**Build:** Lean, weather-scarred, left-handed
**Home:** Durnhollow, a hamlet of forty hearths on the upper Vael Reach
**Bond:** Walking-with partnership with Ashka, a seven-year-old Vael grey wolf

Corin is the lens through which the reader enters Aurelith. Everything extraordinary about this world must first pass through his ordinary, calloused hands. He is not chosen, prophesied, or special — he is competent, stubborn, and incapable of walking past someone who is dying. That single reflex pulls him off the margins and into the buried architecture of a world he thought he understood.

---

## History

Corin was raised in Durnhollow by his father **Alden Ashmark**, a hunter with a reputation for wandering too far north and talking to stones. When Corin was twelve, Alden walked into the high passes and did not return. He left behind debts, a worn skinning knife, and silence where an explanation should have been. The village considers Alden dead. Corin considers him *gone*, which is different — gone implies a direction, and directions can be followed.

After Alden's disappearance, Corin took over his father's trap lines and taught himself the upper Reach through years of solitary work. He trades pelts at **Greywater Market** once a season, maintains civil but distant relations with Durnhollow, and spends more time above the village than inside it. His bond with Ashka formed during his late teens — months of shared territory in the high timber before the wolf simply stopped leaving.

---

## Skills and Knowledge

- **Tracking and hunting:** Corin reads terrain the way literate people read text — shale displacement, scat age, wind-bent lichen, the logic of elk migration through seasonal corridors. He can field-dress a buck in the time it takes to boil water.
- **Improvised survival:** Shelter-building, basic herbcraft learned from necessity, fire management in wet conditions, emergency wound care with pine resin and spider silk. Nothing formal — everything field-tested.
- **The turn:** Like all experienced Reach hunters, Corin navigates by seasonal instinct — the copper of birch leaves, the iron smell of creek mud before spring melt, the quality of silence before snowfall. He does not think of this as skill. It is simply how the world speaks to those who stay quiet long enough.
- **What he lacks:** Literacy beyond trade-marks. Any framework for understanding the phenomena he encounters. Political awareness. The vocabulary to describe what the Pull is doing to him as the story progresses.

---

## Personality

Corin is terse, observational, and dry under pressure. He does not volunteer information, does not pray at the Votive Stones, and does not trust anyone who speaks in certainties. His humor surfaces in understatement — the kind of wit that sounds like a statement of fact until you catch the edge.

His defining trait is **reflexive loyalty to immediate suffering** — not idealism or heroism, but a bone-deep inability to look away from someone bleeding in front of him. This is what makes him rescue Wren. It is also what will cost him, because the world he steps into does not reward compassion with safety.

- **Slow to trust.** Earned trust is permanent; declared trust is suspect.
- **Avoids obligation.** Any tie that might root him — to the village, to debt, to another person's expectations — triggers a quiet withdrawal. He does not recognize this as grief-avoidance. It is.
- **Carries his father like an open wound.** Corin does not talk about Alden. He carries Alden's skinning knife. He hunts Alden's territory. He avoids the high passes where Alden vanished — not from fear, but from a refusal to confirm what he suspects. This unexamined grief is the emotional fault line the story will eventually crack open.

---

## The Wolf-Bond

Corin's walking-with bond with Ashka is genuine — not the exaggerated frontier boast most hunters trade in. The bond is nonverbal, built on shared territory and mutual respect. Ashka does not obey commands; she cooperates when the plan makes sense to her. Their communication runs through body language, breath patterns, and years of reading each other's responses to terrain.

The bond sets Corin apart in Durnhollow. Wolf-bonded hunters are regarded with admiration and unease — an attunement to the land that most respect but few envy. A walking-with hunter is welcome at any hearth but rarely pressed to stay. Corin's isolation is partly temperament, partly the space the village instinctively gives him.

**Critically:** Ashka senses what Corin's rational mind filters out. Her hackles, her refusal to cross certain thresholds, her fixation on Wren during fever spikes — these are the reader's early instruments for the world's strangeness. The wolf reacts first. Always.

---

## Narrative Function

Corin is the story's anchor to the lived, physical, sensory experience of Aurelith. Every revelation about the planet's engineered nature, every encounter with the Substrate's waking memory, must be filtered through his hunter's pragmatism before it reaches the reader. He does not theorize. He notices. He survives. He adapts.

The tension in Corin's arc is between the small life he has built — solitary, competent, sufficient — and the larger truth pulling him toward the interior his father entered and never left. Wren is the catalyst. The cylinder is the question. But the real force is Corin's unspoken suspicion that Alden was not mad, that the stones are not furniture, and that the Pull he has spent his adult life ignoring has been waiting for him to stop.

---

## Writing Guidelines

- **Ground every scene in his senses.** Cold, smoke, the weight of a pack, the ache of a winter knee. Corin lives in his body. Abstract knowledge arrives through physical experience or not at all.
- **Voice is terse and precise.** Short sentences. Concrete nouns. Metaphors drawn from terrain and weather, never from books or theology.
- **Show competence without exposition.** Corin doesn't explain what he's doing — he does it, and the reader infers skill from the efficiency.
- **Let him be wrong.** His pragmatism is a strength and a blind spot. He dismisses things he should examine. He trusts his senses in a world where his senses are receiving signals he has no framework to interpret.
- **Ashka is his shadow-self on the page.** When Corin suppresses a reaction, Ashka expresses it. When Corin rationalizes, Ashka growls.

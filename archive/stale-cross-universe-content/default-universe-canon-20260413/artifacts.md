# Artifacts

## Frontier-Craft Tools

### The Travois

A drag-frame built from two birch poles lashed at the narrow end with strips torn from a cloak, spread wide at the base, with a rough platform of crossbars slung between them. The birch is green — cut live, because dead timber snaps under load on uneven ground. The lashing is whatever is at hand: cloak hem, belt leather, bootlace, stripped bark. Nothing about it is permanent. It is built to survive one task and then become firewood.

Corin builds the travois to move Wren out of the creek bed where he finds him, two days north of the tree line. The terrain between there and Durnhollow is shale scree, dwarf willow thickets, and loose-packed game trails — no route that allows a man to be carried on a back for more than an hour without the carrier's knees giving out. The travois distributes the weight across the ground instead of across a spine. Ashka cannot pull it — she is not a draft animal and the harness geometry is wrong for a wolf's shoulders — but she can break trail ahead while Corin drags.

**Construction notes:**
- **Frame:** Two birch poles, roughly eight feet, trimmed of branches but not debarked (bark adds grip for lashing)
- **Lashing:** Cloak strips wound wet and tightened as they dry; a single belt buckle used as a tension anchor at the apex
- **Platform:** Three crossbars of dead spruce, lighter but brittle, seated in notches hacked with a skinning knife
- **Bedding:** The remainder of the cloak, folded double, to keep the sick man's skin off raw wood
- **Load limit:** One gaunt adult, poorly. The crossbars flex. The frame lists. It is not a good travois. It is the travois that was possible.

### What the Travois Reveals

Frontier people do not accumulate tools. The Vael Reach economy runs on what you can carry, build from what's around you, and abandon without regret. A hunter's permanent kit is small: knife, fire steel, cord, a hand-axe if they're prosperous. Everything else is situational — shelters, snares, drying racks, travois — built at point of need from local material, used until the need passes, left to rot.

This is not poverty, though lowlanders often mistake it for such. It is an adaptation to terrain that punishes weight and rewards improvisation. A Compact surveyor like Davan Thel arrives with polished instruments in fitted cases and considers himself equipped. Corin arrives with a knife and considers the forest equipped.

The distinction matters because it shapes how frontier people relate to *found objects*. A lowlander encountering one of the strange smooth-surfaced artifacts from the deep interior would evaluate it as a thing — what is it made of, what is it worth, where does it belong in a catalogue. A frontier hunter evaluates it as a circumstance — is it useful, is it dangerous, should I leave it alone. Corin's instinct when he finds something he doesn't understand is not curiosity but assessment: *does this change what I need to do next.*

### Frontier Improvisation as Cultural Marker

The quality of improvised tools marks a hunter's competence more reliably than any other signal in the Reach. The travois Corin builds is functional but rough — he is a capable hunter, not an exceptional craftsman. A better woodsman would have used green willow withes for lashing instead of cloak strips (stronger wet, more flexible). An old-timer would have angled the poles differently to ride better over scree.

These details are legible to anyone raised in the Reach. Maren Doss could look at the travois and read Corin's skill level, his hurry, his material constraints, and probably the altitude at which he built it — birch and spruce together means upper tree line, the transition zone where the forest thins. Davan Thel would see sticks tied together.

**Common improvised tools of the Vael Reach:**
- **Travois** — drag-frame for moving meat, timber, or injured people
- **Smoke cradle** — green-wood rack for preserving strips of game over a low fire
- **Snare sets** — cord-and-sapling spring traps, dismantled after each run
- **Lean-to shelters** — angled timber frames with bough thatch, good for one storm
- **Cairn markers** — stacked stones for trail-finding; the only improvised structure meant to last

### The Permanent Kit

The improvised tools define the day. The permanent tools define the hunter. A frontier man's kit — knife, fire steel, cord, a hand-axe if he's lucky — is the only property he does not build at need and abandon without regret. These are carried, maintained, inherited, traded in debt settlements. They are the closest thing to wealth in the Vael Reach, and the closest thing to identity a hunter wears.

#### Alden's Skinning Knife

The knife Corin carries is Alden Ashmark's — left behind with the debts and the silence when Alden walked into the high passes and did not come back. It is the most valuable object Corin owns, though he would not describe it that way.

**Physical description:**
- **Blade:** Blue-grey steel, honed thin from years of use, holding an edge longer than any trade-iron knife in the Reach. The color is distinctive — not the dull charcoal of lowland foundry steel or the orange-tinged iron smelted in frontier bloomeries, but a clean blue-grey that catches light without flashing. In direct sun the surface carries a faint grain, almost a wood-like texture in the metal itself; in overcast or firelight it reads as flat, cold, featureless. A thumb drawn along the flat finds something trade-iron doesn't have — a density, a smoothness that frontier sharpening stones should have scratched rough long ago but haven't. Whether the steel was Alden's work or acquired from somewhere else, Corin has never established. He has never seen its like anywhere else in the Reach.
- **Handle:** Elk antler — identifiable by the tight, straight grain and the pale cream color weathered to tawny brown at the surface. Shaped to fit a hand and worn smooth at the grip from decades of use, the antler has taken on a near-polished quality where Alden's palm and then Corin's pressed against it through thousands of hours of work. The antler is not decorative. It is warm in cold weather where metal handles leech heat from fingers, and it does not slip when slicked with blood or tallow — the porous grain wicks moisture rather than letting it sheet. This is a craftsman's choice, not an aesthetic one. Against Corin's calloused hand, the handle disappears; he does not feel it as a separate object, only as the beginning of the cut.
- **Construction:** Full-tang — the blue-grey steel runs unbroken from the tip of the blade through the entire length of the elk antler handle, peened over a small cap of the same steel at the butt end. The cap sits flush against the antler's crown grain, a cold blue-grey disc the size of a thumbnail set into pale tawny bone — the only point where the knife's two materials meet visibly, and the cleanest metalwork on the entire tool. No rivets, no wrap, no ornamentation. The full tang through antler rather than wood gives the knife a forward balance that favors draw cuts over chopping — a skinner's geometry, not a fighting knife's. The join between blade and antler is tight and has never loosened — not from the cold that splits lowland knife handles every winter, not from the repeated wet-dry cycling of field dressing in rain, not from the thousands of impacts transmitted through antler to steel when the heel strikes bone. Elk antler absorbs shock better than wood and resists splitting along the grain where a hardwood handle would have cracked years ago; whoever chose the material knew what the knife would be asked to do. The whole knife has the quality of something made once, made right, and expected to outlast its owner.

**Wear patterns:** The blade has been resharpened hundreds of times and is narrower now than when it was forged — the belly has crept upward, changing the geometry from a general-purpose skinner to something more precise, almost surgical. Run a finger along the spine and you feel the thinning: a blade that once had the heft to split cartilage now has the delicacy to follow a tendon line. The edge near the heel is nicked from being used on materials harder than flesh: notching the spruce crossbars of the travois, scraping bark, prying frozen latches. These are misuses that Alden would not have tolerated, but Corin is a hunter, not a craftsman, and the knife is a tool before it is an heirloom. Even so — even nicked, even resharpened thin — the blue-grey steel holds its edge longer than it has any right to. Corin hones it out of habit more than necessity.

**What the knife reveals:** Alden made things well. The skinning knife is not frontier-standard — it is better than anything available in Greywater Market, better than anything the trade caravans carry in from the lowlands. The steel, the joinery, the balance all suggest either access to materials and techniques beyond the Reach or a skill that Alden never passed on and never explained. This is consistent with the man who talked to stones and walked into the passes: someone who knew more than his station accounted for.

The handle is frontier-legible — elk antler is common hunting material, the kind of choice any experienced Reach hunter would make. The steel is not. The knife lives in that gap: familiar enough in the hand that Corin never questions it, strange enough in its material that anyone who works metal would. It sits at the intersection of the permanent kit (the practical identity of a hunter) and the mystery of Alden (the unasked questions Corin carries). It is also, quietly, the first suggestion that Alden's relationship to the land's hidden infrastructure was not merely superstitious — a man does not forge blue-grey steel from a frontier bloomery, and a man who could source that steel but dressed it in plain elk antler was building something meant to pass without comment.

**Writing guidelines:**
- The knife is always *the knife* or *the skinning knife* to Corin — never "his father's knife" in narration. He does not sentimentalize it. It is the best tool he has. That it was Alden's is a fact, not a feeling.
- The blue-grey color should be noted once, early, and then left alone. Let the reader hold it.
- When Corin uses the knife, describe the action — the grip, the angle, the resistance of the material — not the object. The knife disappears into competence. It reappears as an object only when someone else notices it: Wren studying the blade, Maren recognizing the steel, Davan cataloguing the craftsmanship with professional interest.
- Never explain the steel. Let it sit alongside the Votive Stones and the Cylinder as another thing that is slightly better than it should be.

### Relationship to Permanent Artifacts

The Votive Stones — carved from non-native rock, inscribed with marks that resist weathering — represent the opposite end of a spectrum from the travois. Frontier people live alongside both without seeing a contradiction. The Stones have always been there; the travois was built this morning. One is sacred furniture, the other is a solved problem. The gap between them — between birch poles and engineered stone that shouldn't exist — is the gap the story lives in, and the one Corin has never thought to question.

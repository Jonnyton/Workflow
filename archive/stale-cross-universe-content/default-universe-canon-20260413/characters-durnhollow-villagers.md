# Durnhollow Villagers

The people who survived the grey soldiers' raid and gathered in the timber hall. These are not adventurers or players in the wider political game — they are the human texture of a frontier community under fracture. Their presence gives weight to Durnhollow as a place worth defending and a loss worth grieving.

---

## Torben — The Farrier

**Role:** Craftsman, practical anchor among the survivors
**Age:** Mid-forties
**Trade:** Farrier — shoes the mules and draft horses that haul charcoal loads south to Greywater Market

Torben is the kind of man a frontier village cannot function without. Farriery in the upper Reach is not a specialized luxury; it is the difference between a working pack train and a lame animal on a mountain switchback. He maintains the forge on the village's east side, a squat stone building that doubles as Durnhollow's only metalworking facility — fixing hinge pins, straightening trap springs, repairing the iron lantern brackets on the ford path.

After the raid, Torben organized the burial detail. He and the Vessen boys dug graves behind the forge before the ground froze too hard to break. This is the act that defines him in crisis: not leadership through words, but leadership through the willingness to do the worst job first. He asks Corin about Wren — and accepts Corin's lie about finding a traveler on the lower trail without pushing, because Torben reads people the way he reads iron: by what they do under heat, not what they claim at rest.

- **Resonance note:** Decades at the forge have bonded the ambient cells deeply to his hands and tools. His work is better than it should be — consistent, precise, almost intuitive. He would call this experience. It is more than that.

## Silla — The Beekeeper

**Role:** Village elder, keeper of the south slope apiaries
**Age:** Late sixties or early seventies — old for the frontier
**Trade:** Beekeeper

Old Silla keeps hives on the south-facing slope above the village, where the wildflower meadows catch enough sun to sustain colonies through the short Reach summer. Honey and beeswax are Durnhollow's only luxury export — Silla's comb fetches premium trade at Greywater because it carries a floral character that lowland apiaries cannot replicate. She has never explained her methods and deflects questions with the cheerful opacity of someone who considers her craft self-evident.

Silla is among the survivors in the timber hall, which means the soldiers either passed over her slope or she was canny enough to stay hidden. At her age, in a village that has just lost a third of its people, she represents continuity — the living memory of Durnhollow before the Arrish families arrived, before Alden vanished, before the world contracted to sixty hearths clinging to a river bend.

- **Resonance note:** Her bees thrive beyond reasonable expectation at this altitude. The hives on the south slope sit near a minor cell-concentration node — not as dense as the Votive Stones, but enough that the colonies benefit from accelerated health and communication. Silla's long, patient presence among them has created a bond she experiences as "listening to the hum." She is closer to understanding the land's living memory than anyone in the village except perhaps Father Edden, and unlike Edden, she has never tried to frame it in doctrine.

## The Harken Children

**Role:** Silent witnesses — the raid's cost made visible
**Ages:** Roughly six to twelve (three or four children in the cluster)
**Family name:** Harken

The Harkens are a frontier family of Arrish descent — the surname carries the hard consonant structure of the highland pastoral clans that folded into Durnhollow two generations back. The children sit in a cluster near the east hearth of the timber hall with the blank, over-still expressions of young things in shock. They do not cry. They do not move toward the adults for comfort. They have drawn into themselves the way children do when the world has done something they have no framework to process.

Their parents are not in the hall. Whether marched north in the soldiers' column or buried behind the forge is not yet clear, and no one in the timber hall is forcing that clarity on them tonight.

- **Narrative function:** The Harken children are the emotional ground truth of the raid. Statistics — thirty survivors from sixty hearths — are abstract. A cluster of children sitting too still near a fire, waiting for parents who will not come, is not. They are the weight that prevents Corin from treating Wren's secrets as an abstract puzzle.

## Maren's Eldest Boy

**Name:** Not yet spoken aloud
**Role:** The lantern-lighter — a duty and its absence
**Age:** Approximately twelve or thirteen

Maren's eldest son has lit the iron lanterns on the ford path every evening since he was nine, performing the task with the excessive seriousness of a child trusted with fire. The detail matters because the lanterns are Durnhollow's heartbeat signal — visible from a mile up the switchbacks, they tell returning hunters that the village is alive. When Corin descends and finds the brackets dark, the boy's absence from his duty is the first concrete sign that something has broken.

Whether the boy survived the raid is unconfirmed. Maren does not mention him during her briefing to Corin, which is itself a kind of answer — or a kind of deferral, from a woman holding the village together by will alone.

---

## The Vessen Boys

**Mentioned in passing:** Torben's burial detail included "the Vessen boys," young men from one of the village's established families. The Vessen household keeps livestock pens near the outer ring of houses — it was a Vessen goat that a mountain cat took three winters ago, the incident Corin remembers when Ashka presses tight to his leg on the descent. The boys are old enough to dig frozen ground and young enough that Torben chose them for the task, which suggests mid-to-late teens and the frontier expectation that proximity to death is not something you shield young people from.

## Village Dynamics After the Raid

The survivors in the timber hall have moved past the first hours of crisis into what Corin recognizes as the grim machinery of survival: bedrolls between bench rows, salvaged food stores stacked along the far wall, organizational logic replacing grief as the operating system. Maren is the de facto leader — not elected, not appointed, but present and loud enough (usually) and practical enough (always) to fill the vacuum that sixty hearths compressed into one room creates.

The village's social fabric has been cut along unpredictable lines. The soldiers took people who couldn't answer their questions about the high passes and killed those who resisted. This means the survivors are not a random cross-section — they are the people who either hid, cooperated, or were lucky enough to be overlooked. That selection pressure will shape Durnhollow's recovery in ways that haven't surfaced yet.

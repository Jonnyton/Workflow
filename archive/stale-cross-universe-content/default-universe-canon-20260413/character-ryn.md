# Ryn Ashmark — Frontier Hunter of Durnhollow

## Role

Protagonist and point-of-view character for Book I. Ryn is the starving hunter who rescues the dying stranger in Chapter 1 — the inciting act that pulls him onto the first hidden rung of Aurelith's buried truth.

## Identity

Ryn is a man in his late teens, the settlement's primary hunter and one of its few members willing to range deep into the upper gorge country alone. He carries mixed heritage: his father came from the wolf-tradition peoples of the interior; his mother from a darker-skinned lineage the frontier communities don't name aloud. Neither bloodline grants him status. Both grant him skills the village depends on without acknowledging.

His family name is **Ashmark** — his father's name, carried but rarely spoken. In Durnhollow he is simply Ryn: the hunter, the one who comes back with meat or doesn't come back at all. The surname surfaces in other people's mouths, not his own — in the careful pause before Maren changes the subject, in the way older hunters glance at the high passes when someone says the word. Ryn does not use it because it names an absence he has not resolved.

## Physical Presence

The prose introduces him through economy of motion rather than physical description. What emerges: cracked calluses from years of bowwork, hands that know their trade past the point of conscious thought, a body that has learned to convert exhaustion into function. By the end of the opening scene he has gone four days on bad food and no real sleep. He collapses within sight of the village after hauling a travois through a full day of ridge country in worsening snow.

He is not large. He survives by reading the land faster than it changes — tracking, foraging, building shelter and tools from whatever the forest offers.

## Core Traits

- **Selfless to the point of self-destruction.** He abandons a clean kill on the only deer the village has seen in eleven days to follow Ash's warning upstream. He finds the dying stranger and never hesitates — builds the travois, dresses the wound, hauls the weight. The village's hunger is real to him; the stranger's death is more immediate.
- **Dry, practical, interior.** Hours of silence broken by a single wry line to a man who can't hear him. No self-pity. No heroic framing. He does what the situation requires and does the math on what it costs.
- **Body-memory over book learning.** His father taught him by walking and pointing and expecting Ryn to remember — the interior traditions passed not through language but through shared motion across difficult terrain. His mother — dead since he was six or seven — knew plants. Ryn carries her knowledge in his hands: pine resin for wounds, usnea lichen for infection, birch bark tea for fever. He doesn't name the tradition. He just uses it.

## The Wolf — Ash

Ryn's female wolf companion. He found her as a pup in a frozen den, small enough to carry in one hand. She is silver-grey, dusted white where her breath crystallizes against her muzzle, and she operates with the same economy Ryn does — no wasted motion, no wasted attention. Her ears track independently, reading the forest in stereo.

Ash is hunting partner, travois-breaker, and emotional anchor. In the opening scene she makes the critical decision: she turns away from the deer and toward the ambush site upstream, reading something in the wind Ryn cannot name. When she finds the dying stranger, she lies beside him without growling — a judgment call that Ryn follows without question.

Their bond is the emotional spine of Book I. It is not spoken. It is built from years of shared cold, shared hunger, and the silent language of a hunter who trusts his wolf's nose more than his own eyes.

## His Father — Alden Ashmark

Alden Ashmark was an interior man who settled in Durnhollow — or rather, who stopped moving long enough for Durnhollow to form habits around him. He came from the wolf-tradition peoples of the high country, married Ryn's mother, hunted the upper Reach, and carried a reputation the village never quite knew what to do with. He talked to stones. Not in the way Father Edden talks to the Votive shrines — not prayer, not ritual — but in the way a man talks to something he expects to answer. The village found this unsettling. Alden found the village's discomfort irrelevant.

He vanished into the high passes when Ryn was twelve, leaving behind debts, a worn skinning knife, and the kind of silence that fills a house differently than absence. One of the upper passes is still called **Alden's Gap** — named not because he discovered it but because he was the last person to walk through it and not come back. The village considers Alden dead. Ryn considers him gone, which is different.

What Alden was doing in the high passes — what he was looking for, or looking *at* — is an open wound in Ryn's understanding of his own life. The skills Alden passed down (tracking, reading weather across shale, the interior traditions of walking-with) were taught through demonstration, not explanation. Ryn knows *how* his father moved through the world but not *why* he moved toward the places that frightened everyone else.

Alden features in several frontier **ash-tales** — the grim, cautionary stories told around hearth fires about people who went somewhere they shouldn't have. The versions told in Durnhollow are notably kinder than those told elsewhere. Ryn has heard both kinds. He does not correct the unkind ones.

**Maren** knew Alden. She has never told Ryn what Alden was actually doing in the high passes, and Ryn has never asked the right question.

## Knowledge Boundaries

Ryn's lived experience reads as high fantasy. He knows the Ashwater, the timber palisade, the healer Maren, the farrier Torben. He knows the land is harder than it used to be and the herds move wrong. He does not know why. The deeper architecture of Aurelith — the engineered cells, the buried technology, the false divinity — remains beneath his horizon. His proximity to these truths (through heritage, through the stranger he rescues, through skills that echo older knowledge) is felt as instinct and tradition, never as understanding.

His father's legacy makes this proximity sharper. Alden walked toward the Deep Interior deliberately; Ryn was raised in the wake of that choice. The skinning knife Alden left behind is the only artifact Ryn carries from his father — he does not know that the high passes Alden walked through hold structures and surfaces that make the knife's simple iron feel like a different century.
